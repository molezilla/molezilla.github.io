Our Results

 

Our group's findings indicate that yes, Amazon was in the right to do away with review incentives. Overall, incentivized received 4.55 stars, whereas non-incentivized reviews received 4.12. There is a clear and consistent bias between incentivized and non-incentivized reviews, which can be seen below.

 



We broke the reviews up further into standard and 5-core review sets, for better analysis. 5-core review sets contained only items which had 5 or more reviews, and only reviewers who had rated 5 or more products. These were a small proportion of the review data. As one can clearly see, the vast majority of the data was generated by users or sellers with only 1-4 reviews. Further, the statisticsfor 5-core versus 1-core data for each item subset (clothing, health, etc) were largely consistent, as evidenced by the chart above.



 

 

The bias here is clear- Our group is unable to speak for the psychological or economical reasons why, but there is a clear difference between ratings for incentivized and non-incentivized reviews. This means that the system is inaccurate, and Amazon was right to take actions to stop review incentives.

Since the proportions were too small, our group was unable to find a way to visually represent the data for our next point. However, we found that the proportion of 5-core users who were offered incentives was disproportionately high- for example, in Health and Personal Care, the 5-core review set showed .06% of reviews as incentivized, whereas the general population showed .01%. This held true for all review sets, showing from 3 to 10 times the incidence of incentives in the 5-core versus the general population.
